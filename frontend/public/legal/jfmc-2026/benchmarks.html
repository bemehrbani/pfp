Your Position in Context

Based on the current legal framework, People for Peace & Justice ry is:

  • A small, newly-formed registered association in Finland
  • Focused on legal research, evidence documentation, and case-building for a specific humanitarian disaster
  • Aiming to hold a state institution accountable through international justice mechanisms (ICC, ICJ, universal jurisdiction)
  • Starting with 3 founders, minimal budget, and a digital-first approach
Good news: This profile is not unusual — several of the most impactful accountability organizations started in exactly this way.

Tier 1 — Direct Benchmarks

These organizations match PFP's profile most closely: founded by small groups of activists to document and build legal cases around specific humanitarian crises.

1 TRIAL International

Swiss NGO · Geneva Est. 2002 ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
Founded by 20 human rights activists, lawyers, and torture victims Origin trigger The Pinochet arrest precedent (1998) — wanted to bring similar cases in Switzerland First 5 years No budget, no staff — all volunteer work Turning point 2007: First funding, first staff hired, first field office (Sarajevo) Now Major international justice NGO with cases at ICC, UN treaty bodies, and national courts

What they did early: Launched a multilingual website ("Trial Watch"), published legal manuals on using Swiss law against impunity, filed criminal complaints in Swiss courts under universal jurisdiction, organized conferences.

Key Lesson for PFP

TRIAL operated for 5 years on zero budget with only volunteer activists. Their growth proves that the research and documentation phase can be bootstrapped before external funding arrives.

2 Commission for International Justice & Accountability (CIJA)

Non-profit NGO Est. 2011 ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
Founded by Dr. William Wiley (former war crimes investigator) Origin trigger Syrian civil war — urgent need to preserve evidence before it was lost Core method Deploying investigators into conflict zones to collect regime documents Evidence 1.2 million+ internal Syrian government documents; 5,500+ witness interviews Impact Evidence used to convict Eyad Al-Gharib and Anwar Ruslan in Germany (crimes against humanity)
Key Lesson for PFP

CIJA was purpose-built for one specific conflict (Syria). They developed prosecution-grade evidence-gathering that national courts later relied on. PFP's focus on the Minab case follows the same model. Now supports prosecutions in 16 countries.

3 Abdorrahman Boroumand Center (ABC)

US 501(c)(3) · Washington DC Est. 2001 ★ CLOSEST PARALLEL
Founded by Ladan & Roya Boroumand (sisters of an assassinated Iranian activist) Origin trigger 1991 assassination of Dr. Abdorrahman Boroumand by Iranian state agents in Paris Started as Home office in Washington DC Core project "Omid" database — 27,000+ profiles of individuals whose right to life was violated by Iran Method Rigorous "do no harm" verification: first-hand testimonies, eyewitness accounts, official records, cross-referenced

What PFP should study: Multi-language accessibility (Farsi + English), remain independent and apolitical, pure documentation focus that became the definitive resource for researchers, courts, and media.

Key Lesson for PFP

This is the closest parallel — a small, diaspora-founded non-profit focused specifically on documenting Iranian state violence. Their Omid database became a definitive resource. PFP's documentation of the 168+ Minab victims follows the exact same approach.

4 Syrian Network for Human Rights (SNHR)

Independent Non-profit Est. 2011 ⭐⭐⭐⭐
Founded by Fadel Abdul Ghany Origin trigger Syrian uprising — immediate need to document government violence Core method Real-time violation documentation; named victim databases Impact Became primary data source for UN investigations, media, and courts
Key Lesson for PFP

Started as a small documentation effort during an active crisis, grew into the authoritative source that international bodies relied upon. Their named-victim approach mirrors PFP's "name every child" strategy.

5 Syrian Archive

Non-profit Collective Est. 2011 ⭐⭐⭐⭐
Founded by A collective of Syrian human rights activists Core method Curating and preserving visual documentation (video, photos) of violations Impact Evidence-based tool for reporting, advocacy, and court-admissible evidence
Key Lesson for PFP

Pioneered digital/social media evidence archiving for legal accountability — directly applicable to PFP's satellite imagery and media preservation strategy.

Tier 2 — Structural Models

Larger organizations that started similarly and can serve as growth models.

6 Global Rights Compliance (GRC)

Stichting · The Hague Est. 2013 ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
Founded by Wayne Jordash QC & Scott Martin Focus International criminal, humanitarian, and human rights law in conflict zones Operations Syria, Iraq, Ukraine, Yemen Divisions Starvation & Humanitarian Crisis Division · Gender & Child Justice Division
Key Lesson for PFP

GRC is essentially what PFP could become — a small Hague-based foundation that grew into a full-service international legal practice. Their Stichting structure is the exact model for PFP's planned Netherlands secondary entity.

7 Physicians for Human Rights (PHR)

US Non-profit Est. 1986 ⭐⭐⭐⭐
Founded by 5 physicians Innovation Using forensic science as evidence for human rights accountability Achievements Chemical weapons in Iraq, mass graves in Bosnia & Rwanda, ICC tribunal evidence Recognition Nobel Peace Prize co-recipient (1997)
Key Lesson for PFP

PHR proved that specialized expertise applied to human rights documentation creates evidence courts cannot dismiss. PFP's equivalent could be digital forensics (satellite imagery analysis, social media verification).

8 HANA Human Rights Organization

Iran-focused ⭐⭐⭐⭐
Focus Documenting Iranian state violence against children specifically Key work Confirmed identities of 148 children killed during 2022 protests Method Document analysis, multi-source verification, forensic-grade identification
Key Lesson for PFP

HANA is doing for the 2022 protests what PFP is doing for Minab — identifying and naming each child victim individually. Potential partner organization.

Key Patterns Across All Benchmarks

What successful accountability NGOs have in common:

  • Started by a few passionate individuals
    TRIAL (20 activists), ABC (2 sisters), CIJA (1 investigator), PHR (5 doctors)
  • Bootstrapped for years
    TRIAL had no budget for 5 years; ABC started from a home office
  • Focused on documentation first, litigation later
    Evidence preservation is always Phase 1
  • Named every victim
    ABC's Omid database (27,000+), SNHR's victim lists, HANA's 148 children
  • Built to court-admissible standards
    CIJA's methodology is explicitly prosecution-grade
  • Leveraged digital tools early
    Syrian Archive, SNHR, PFP's Telegram bot approach
  • Remained independent and apolitical
    Credibility depends on not being aligned with any state
  • Engaged international legal mechanisms
    ICC, ICJ, universal jurisdiction, UN treaty bodies
  • Grew through partnerships
    CICC membership, ECCHR collaboration, joint filings

The Typical Growth Arc

Year 0–1
Found org → Document everything → Build evidence archive
Year 1–2
First partnerships → Join coalitions (CICC) → File first complaints
Year 2–5
Secure first funding → Hire staff → Expand operations
Year 5+
Become authoritative source → Courts cite evidence → Scale impact
PFP is at Year 0. The benchmarks show this is the critical evidence-preservation phase. The organizations that succeeded did so because they started documenting immediately, even before having formal structure, funding, or staff.
