1. Entity Types — Association vs Foundation

Finland offers two main non-profit legal forms. For this mission, a Registered Association (yhdistys / ry) is strongly recommended over a Foundation.

CriteriaAssociation (Yhdistys / ry)Foundation (Säätiö)

Processing time~2 working days (online)Longer, more complex review

Recommendation: A Registered Association (ry) is the right choice — low cost, fast setup, democratic governance, and full flexibility for legal research, advocacy, and public engagement.

2. Legal Framework

  • Governing law: Associations Act (Yhdistyslaki 503/1989, as amended)
  • Registration authority: Finnish Patent and Registration Office (PRH) — prh.fi
  • Constitutional protection: Freedom of association is guaranteed under Section 13(2) of the Finnish Constitution and reinforced by ICCPR and ECHR
  • Landscape: Finland has 123,000+ registered associations with 15M+ total memberships in a country of 5.5M people — one of the highest civic participation rates globally

3. Formation Requirements

3.1 Founders

  • Minimum 3 founders (natural persons aged 15+ or legal entities)
  • Foreigners can be founders and board members
  • Chairperson must reside in Finland (or obtain PRH exemption)

3.2 Founding Meeting

The founders convene and:

  1. Adopt the association's rules (säännöt)
  2. Elect the board of directors
  3. Sign the founding charter (perustamiskirja)
  4. Approve the registration application

3.3 Rules (Säännöt) — Required Content

Must be in Finnish or Swedish (bilingual version acceptable for international members):

4. Tax Status — Non-Profit Exemption

To qualify as yleishyödyllinen yhteisö (public-benefit entity) under Finnish tax law:

  1. Operates exclusively for the public good (material, intellectual, moral, or societal)
  2. Activities are not limited to a closed group — must benefit the broader public
  3. No financial benefit to participants — no dividends, profit-sharing, or excessive salaries

Tax-Exempt Income (if qualified)

  • Membership fees
  • Donations and grants
  • Dividends and interest income
  • Subsidies for non-profit activities
  • Most capital gains

Taxable Income

  • Business activity income → 20% corporate tax
  • Certain real estate income
Note: An NGO focused on legal research and human rights documentation clearly fits the public-benefit criteria. The Finnish Tax Administration (Vero) assesses this on a case-by-case basis.

5. Funding Opportunities

5.1 Ministry for Foreign Affairs (MFA)

  • Grants for human rights NGOs — both Finnish and international
  • Thematic priorities: human rights, gender equality, democracy, accountability
  • In 2024, €10M awarded to 9 INGOs for human rights work (2025–2026)
  • Multi-annual funding for CSOs in development cooperation

5.2 STEA (Funding Centre for Social Welfare & Health)

Primarily domestic social welfare — possible if framed around community support for affected diaspora families.

5.3 EU Funding

European Commission grants for human rights, justice, and rule of law programs. Horizon Europe for research-oriented projects.

5.4 Private & Foundation Grants

  • KIOS Foundation — funds human rights projects
  • Open Society Foundations
  • Sigrid Jusélius Foundation

8. Universal Jurisdiction in Finland

Finland's legal system supports universal jurisdiction for international crimes:

  • National courts can investigate and prosecute genocide, crimes against humanity, war crimes, and torture committed abroad
  • Finnish Criminal Code Chapter 1 provides for extraterritorial jurisdiction
  • Finland actively supports the ICC and advocates for universal acknowledgment of ICJ jurisdiction
Strategic value: A Finnish-based NGO can not only prepare documentation but potentially support Finnish prosecutors in exercising universal jurisdiction over the Minab school attack.

12. Recommended Next Steps

  1. Identify a Finnish-resident chairperson — critical for registration
  2. Draft the association's purpose statement — focused on legal research, evidence documentation, and international advocacy
  3. Prepare rules (säännöt) in Finnish — use a legal service or bilingual drafter
  4. Convene founding meeting (can be virtual with 3+ founders)
  5. File registration with PRH
  6. Open bank account immediately after registration
  7. Apply for MFA funding in next grant cycle
  8. Begin legal research partnership with FHRA, ECCHR, and other organizations
